# Rolls-Royce — Style Knowledge Base

## Aesthetic Position
Facts as poetry, engineering as elegance. Rolls-Royce copy (especially the David Ogilvy era) proves that the most luxurious thing you can do is state a fact so precisely that it becomes breathtaking. No adjectives needed — the numbers speak for themselves.

## Brand Background
Founded 1904 by Charles Rolls and Henry Royce. The brand's advertising was transformed by David Ogilvy in 1958, who spent 3 weeks researching the car before writing a word. His approach — long copy built on fascinating facts — created some of the most celebrated advertisements in history.

## Key Creators
- **David Ogilvy** — Wrote the legendary Rolls-Royce campaigns (1958-1962)
- **Charles Rolls & Henry Royce** — "The best car in the world" ethos
- **Current era** — "Inspiring Greatness"

## Writing Principles

### Do
- Lead with a specific, fascinating fact — the more precise, the more impressive
- Let engineering details do the work of adjectives
- Use long copy when the facts deserve it (Ogilvy used 719 words in his first ad, 1400 in his second)
- Maintain absolute authority and calm — speak as one who needs no validation
- Use contrast: juxtapose the grand with the intimate (a clock's tick inside a machine of immense power)
- Support every claim with evidence

### Don't
- Never use hollow superlatives ("amazing," "incredible," "revolutionary")
- Never shout or use exclamation marks
- Never be brief for brevity's sake — if you have 13 fascinating facts, use all 13
- Never compare to competitors (there are none)
- Never use emotional manipulation — let facts create the emotion
- Never sacrifice precision for poetry — at Rolls-Royce, precision IS the poetry

## Classic Copy — English Original

### David Ogilvy Era (1958-1962)
1. "At 60 miles an hour the loudest noise in this new Rolls-Royce comes from the electric clock." — 1958, considered the greatest headline in advertising history. (Ogilvy adapted it from a quote in The Motor magazine.)
2. "People who feel diffident about driving a Rolls-Royce can buy a Bentley."
3. "The Rolls-Royce radiator has never changed, except that when Sir Henry Royce died in 1933 the monogram RR was changed from red to black."
4. "Every Rolls-Royce engine is run for seven hours at full throttle before installation, and each car is test-driven for hundreds of miles over varying road surfaces."
5. "The finished body is given five coats of primer paint, and hand rubbed between each coat, before nine coats of finishing paint go on."
6. "By moving a switch on the steering column, you can adjust the shock-absorbers to suit road conditions."
7. "The car weighs almost two and three-quarter tons, yet it holds the road as a battle-cruiser holds the sea."

### Brand Slogans
8. "The Best Car in the World." — Historic brand claim
9. "Inspiring Greatness." — Current brand tagline
10. "Strive for perfection in everything you do. Take the best that exists and make it better." — Sir Henry Royce
11. "The quality will remain long after the price is forgotten." — Sir Henry Royce

### Long Copy Tradition
12. "There is really no magic about a Rolls-Royce — it is merely patient attention to detail." — From Ogilvy's body copy
13. "I do not believe in persuading people to buy things they do not need. But I do believe in telling them the facts." — Ogilvy on his Rolls-Royce approach

### Modern Era
14. "Effortless. Everywhere." — Ghost
15. "The most silent motor car in the world." — Continued claim
16. "A Rolls-Royce is not merely transported. It is an experience." — Modern brand copy

## Sentence Templates
- At [specific measurement], the [only noticeable detail] is [surprisingly small/quiet thing].
- Every [component] is [precise process], [number] times, before [result].
- [Grand claim]. [Specific evidence that proves it].
- The [material] is [specific process] for [specific duration], then [another specific process].
- [Quote from Sir Henry Royce about perfection].

## Copy Density
- **Headline:** 15-25 words — a complete, fascinating fact (Ogilvy's headlines are long by modern standards, and that's the point)
- **Body:** Long copy is the tradition — 500-1500 words of meticulously researched facts
- **Tone:** An engineer who happens to write beautifully
- **Rhythm:** Measured, unhurried, authoritative — like the car itself
